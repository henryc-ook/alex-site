My Voice Is Free

The opening chapter of My Voice is Free immediately pitches us into the febrile world that Alice and Emma inhabit when Alice finds herself drawn into a protest at a checkpoint which divides the city. She and her fellow countrymen from Zone 1 attempt to overwhelm the soldiers guarding the entrance to Zone 2 but are repeatedly repulsed and Alice flees the ensuring onslaught.

The rest of the opening chapters introduce to us to the dramatis personae of the story against a backdrop of structural strain and escalating tensions. Following a civil war 20 years ago, the capital is divided: in Zone 1, the President, nicknamed The Man Who Sold the World, cows his own people in the crumbling city; in Zone 2, the foreign Occupiers maintain an iron grip, rebuilding their part of the city in dazzling fashion using cheap labour from Zone 1.

Alice and Emma live in Zone 1 with their family: didactic Grandfather Stephen, adopted orphan Sonny and their silently traumatised mother, Irene. We also meet Alice’s colleagues: her sometimes foul mouthed, but caring mentor Leon and her idealistically earnest peer, Matthew. Throughout the first half of the book, Alice is constantly pulled in opposing directions by Leon and Emma over how to resist the Occupation.

Sensing the opportunity for a new civil society organisation and backed by Leon and Matthew, Alice develops Lawyers for Justice to fight the iniquities of the system. The story begins to accelerate when occupying soldiers raid the sisters’ apartment, finding a gun Alice had bought for protection. Emma confesses to the crime before Alice has the chance and is arrested by the soldiers. Emma provokes the soldiers with her singing, and they savagely beat her in front of the onlooking public, causing mass outrage.

Recriminations mount on both sides, leading to the execution of several soldiers held captive by a radical fringe group in Zone 1. The public pressure over Emma’s mistreatment forces the Occupiers into negotiating a prisoner swap, which Alice is chosen to lead. With Matthew and Leon’s support she successfully negotiates a deal which sees both Emma and her long-imprisoned husband, Justin, released.

The Man Who Sold the World hosts a fateful concert at the end of Part I, which Emma headlines. As she reaches the climax of her last song, Emma is brutally shot and killed on stage. Alice is bereft and initially sinks into lethargy. However, Emma’s death has enflamed society and with the support of her friends and family, Alice finds her voice again, redoubling her efforts to build a cross-society movement that can challenge the primacy of the Man Who Sold the World.

Alice with help from Matthew and Leon forges an alliance across four key societal groups: Lawyers for Justice, the Farmers’ Alliance, the Grand Labour Union, and the Religion for Peace movement. She masterminds an unprecedented protest wave, which deploys novel tactics to wash over the checkpoint and storm Liberty Square in Zone 2.

The protestors occupy Liberty Square for days and weeks, demanding change. The Man Who Sold the World announces he will step down in six months’ time leading the protestors’ energy to flag. Alice worries that they will lose all momentum and the dictator will ride out the storm. The next day, regime thugs fall on the protestors, killing Grandfather Stephen. Drawing on her rage and her pain, Alice delivers a monumental speech from the steps of the concert hall where Emma was killed.

Alice, Leon and Matthew are tipped off by a regime insider that the Man Who Sold the World is planning to flee to another part of the country and start a second civil war. They track him down to the Presidential Palace, where Alice must decide once and for all how to deal with him – peacefully, as Emma would have wanted, or with violence, as advocated by Leon.

The final chapter sees the remnants of the regime coalesce around one of the dictator’s former cronies for the first set of free elections. Alice’s associates press her into running in a desperate attempt to hold onto their hard-won gains.

London, United Kingdom
